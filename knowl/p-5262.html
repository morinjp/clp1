We differentiate \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) using the chain rule. Recall \(\ds\diff{}{x}\left\{\arcsin(x)\right\}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} f'(x)&=\frac{2}{\sqrt{1-\textcolor{red}{\sqrt{x}}^2}}\cdot\diff{}{x}\textcolor{red}{\sqrt{x}}\\ &=\frac{2}{\sqrt{1-x}}\cdot\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x-x^2}}\cr g'(x)&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\textcolor{red}{(2x-1)}^2}}\cdot\diff{}{x}\{\textcolor{red}{2x-1}\}\\ &=\frac{2}{\sqrt{1-4x^2+4x-1}}\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x-x^2}}\cr \end{align*}

So, the derivative of \(f(x)-g(x)\) is \(0\text{,}\) which implies that \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) differ by a constant--perhaps a surprising result!
