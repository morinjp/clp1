If that isn't satisfying, you can go through the normal method of calculating \(T_{100}(t)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} s(t)&=4.9t^2-t+10 & s(5)&=4.9(25)-5+10=127.5\\ s'(t)&=9.8t-1 & s'(5)&=9.8(5)-1=48\\ s''(t)&=9.8 & s''(5)&=9.8 \end{align*}

The rest of the derivatives of \(s(t)\) are identically zero, so they are (in particular) zero when \(t=5\text{.}\) Therefore,

\begin{align*} T_{100}(t)&=127.5+48(t-5)+\frac{1}{2}9.8(t-5)^2\\ &=127.5+48(t-5)+4.9(t-5)^2 \end{align*}

We can now check that \(T_{100}(t)\) really is the same as \(s(t)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} T_{100}(t)&=127.5+48(t-5)+4.9(t-5)^2\\ &=127.5+48(t-5)+4.9(t^2-10t+25)\\ &=[127.5+48(-5)+4.9(25)]+[48-4.9(10)]t+4.9t^2\\ &=10-t+4.9t^2=s(t) \end{align*}
