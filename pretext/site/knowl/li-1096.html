Item

[3.4.11.7.a] For every value of \(n\text{,}\) the term being added is simply the constant \(1\text{.}\) So, \(\ds\sum_{n=5}^{30} 1 = 1+1+\cdots+1\text{.}\) The trick is figuring out how many 1s are added. Our index \(n\) takes on all integers from 5 to 30, including 5 and 30, which is 26 numbers. So, \(\ds\sum_{n=5}^{30}=26\text{.}\)

If you're having a hard time seeing why the sum is 26, instead of 25, think of it this way: there are thirty numbers in the collection \(\{1,2,3,4,5,6,\ldots,29,30\}\text{.}\) If we remove the first four, we get \(30-4=26\) numbers in the collection \(\{5,6,\ldots,30\}\text{.}\)
