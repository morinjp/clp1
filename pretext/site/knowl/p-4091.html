We begin by finding the derivatives of \(f\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} f(x)&=(1 - 3x)^{-1/3} & f(0) & = 1\\ f'(x)&=(-3)\frac{-1}{3}(1 - 3x)^{-4/3}=(1-3x)^{-4/3} & f'(0) & = 1\\ f''(x)&=(-3)\frac{-4}{3}(1-3x)^{-7/3}={4}(1 - 3x)^{-7/3} & f''(0) & = 4\\ f^{(3)}(x)&=(-3)(4)\frac{-7}{3}(1-3x)^{-10/3}=28(1 - 3x)^{-10/3} & f^{(3)}(0) & = 28 \end{align*}

Plugging these into the definition of a Taylor Polynomial, we find that the third--order Taylor polynomial for \(f\) around \(x = 0\) is

\begin{align*} T_3(x)&=1+x+\frac{4}{2!}x^2+\frac{28}{3!}x^3\\ &=1 + x +2x^2+\frac{14}{3}x^3 \end{align*}
