Looking at the table in the notes, we see the antiderivative of \(\dfrac{1}{x}\) is \(f(x)=\log|x|+C\text{.}\) The given information \(f(-1)=0\) lets us find \(C\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} 0=f(-1)&=\log|-1|+C\\ 0&=\log(1)+C\\ 0&=C \end{align*}

So, \(f(x)=\log|x|\text{.}\)

Remark: it is true that \(\log x\) is an antiderivative of \(\dfrac{1}{x}\text{,}\) since the derivative of \(\log x\) is \(\dfrac{1}{x}\text{.}\) However, \(\log x\) is only defined for positive values of \(x\text{.}\) Since the given information tells you that \(f(x)\) is defined when \(x=-1\text{,}\) you need to use a more general antiderivative of \(\dfrac{1}{x}\text{:}\) \(\log|x|+C\text{.}\)
