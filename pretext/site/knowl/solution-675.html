Solution 4.1.2.8.1

We know the derivatives of sine and cosine. We'll work from there to build a function whose derivative is \(f(x)\text{.}\) We'll start by finding an antiderivative of \(7\cos(13x)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \diff{}{x}\{\sin x\}&=\cos x\\ \diff{}{x}\{\sin(13x)\}&=13\cos(13x)\\ \diff{}{x}\left\{\frac{1}{13}\sin(13x)\right\}&=\frac{13}{13}\cos(13x)=\cos(13x)\\ \diff{}{x}\left\{\frac{7}{13}\sin(13x)\right\}&=7\cos(13x)\\ \end{align*}

So, an antiderivative of \(7\cos(13x)\) is \(\dfrac{7}{13}\sin(13x)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \diff{}{x}\{\cos x\}&=-\sin x\\ \diff{}{x}\{-\cos x\}&=\sin x\\ \diff{}{x}\{-\cos (5x)\}&=5\sin (5x)\\ \diff{}{x}\left\{-\frac{3}{5}\cos (5x)\right\}&=\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)5\sin (5x)=3\sin(5x) \end{align*}

So, an antiderivative of \(3\sin(5x)\) is \(-\dfrac{3}{5}\cos(5x)\text{.}\)

The most general antiderivative of \(3\sin(5x)+7\cos(13x)\) is

\begin{equation*} F(x)=-\dfrac{3}{5}\cos(5x)+\dfrac{7}{13}\sin(13x)+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
