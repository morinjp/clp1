In Question 3.4.11.1, we differentiated the Taylor polynomial to find its derivative. We don't really want to differentiate this ten times, though, so let's look for another way. Unlike Question 3.4.11.1, our Taylor polynomial is given to us in a form very similar to its definition. The \(n\)th degree Taylor polynomial for \(f(x)\) about \(x=5\) is

\begin{align*} T_n(x)&=\sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(5)}{k!}(x-5)^k\\ \end{align*}

So,

\begin{align*} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(5)}{k!}(x-5)^k&=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{2k+1}{3k-9}(x-5)^k\\ \end{align*}

For any \(k\) from 0 to \(n\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \frac{f^{(k)}(5)}{k!}&=\frac{2k+1}{3k-9}\\ \end{align*}

In particular, when \(k=10\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \frac{f^{(10)}(5)}{10!}&=\frac{20+1}{30-9}=1\\ f^{(10)}(5)&=10! \end{align*}
