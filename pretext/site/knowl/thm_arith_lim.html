Theorem 1.4.3 Arithmetic of limits

Let \(a,c \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) let \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) be defined for all \(x\)'s that lie in some interval about \(a\) (but \(f,g\) need not be defined exactly at \(a\)).

\begin{align*} \lim_{x \to a} f(x)&=F & \lim_{x \to a} g(x) &=G \end{align*}

exist with \(F,G \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then the following limits hold

  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} ( f(x) + g(x) ) = F+G\) — limit of the sum is the sum of the limits.
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} ( f(x) - g(x) ) = F - G\) — limit of the difference is the difference of the limits.
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} c f(x) = c F\text{.}\)
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} ( f(x) \cdot g(x) ) = F \cdot G\) — limit of the product is the product of limits.
  • If \(G \neq 0\) then \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{F}{G}\) and, in particular, \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{1}{g(x)} = \frac{1}{G}\text{.}\)

Note — be careful with this last one — the denominator cannot be zero.
