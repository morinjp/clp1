Solution 1.5.2.25.1

  • Solution 1:

    When \(a\) approaches 0 from the right, the numerator approaches negative infinity, and the denominator approaches \(-1\text{.}\) So, \(\ds\lim_{a \to 0^+}\dfrac{a^2-\frac{1}{a}}{a-1}=\infty\text{.}\)

    More precisely, using Theorem 1.5.9:

    \begin{align*} &\lim_{a \to 0^+} \frac{1}{a}=+\infty\\ \mbox{Also,}& \lim_{a \to 0^+} a^2=0 \end{align*}

    So, using Theorem 1.5.9,

    \begin{align*} & \lim_{a \to 0^+} a^2-\frac{1}{a}=-\infty\\ \text{Furthermore,}&\lim_{a \to 0^+}a-1=-1\\ \text{So, using our theorem,}&\lim_{a \to 0^+}\frac{a^2-\frac{1}{a}}{a-1}=\infty \end{align*}

  • Solution 2:

    Since \(a=0\) is not in the domain of our function, a reasonable impulse is to simplify.

    \begin{align*} \frac{a^2-\frac{1}{a}}{a-1}\left(\frac{a}{a}\right)&=\frac{a^3-1}{a(a-1)}=\frac{(a-1)(a^2+a+1)}{a(a-1)}\\ \end{align*}

    So,

    \begin{align*} \lim_{a \to 0^+}\frac{a^2-\frac{1}{a}}{a-1}&= \lim_{a \to 0^+}\frac{(a-1)(a^2+a+1)}{a(a-1)}\\ &=\lim_{a \to 0^+}\frac{a^2+a+1}{a}\\ &=\lim_{a \to 0^+}a+1+\frac{1}{a}=\infty \end{align*}
