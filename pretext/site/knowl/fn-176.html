Footnote C.0.1
By “\(f(x)\) is strictly increasing” we mean that \(f(a)\lt f(b)\) whenever \(a\lt b\text{.}\) As \(f'(x)\gt 0\) for all \(x\ne-\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) \(f(x)\) is strictly increasing even as \(x\) passes through \(-\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) For example, for any \(x\gt -\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) the mean value theorem (Theorem 2.13.5) tells us that there is a \(c\) strictly between \(-\frac{1}{2}\) and \(x\) such that \(f(x)-f\big(-\frac{1}{2}\big) = f'(c)\big(x+\frac{1}{2}\big)\gt 0\text{.}\)in-context
